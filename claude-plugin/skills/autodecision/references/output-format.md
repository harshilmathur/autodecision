# Decision Brief Template

Use this template for Phase 8 (DECIDE) output. Write to `DECISION-BRIEF.md` in the run directory.

**Framing:** The brief is a possibility map, not a decision memo. It shows what the exploration surfaced (hypotheses, effects, council disagreements, adversarial scenarios, assumptions) and closes with one synthesis at the end. The map is the output. The recommendation is a convenience for readers who need a single direction, not the product.

NOTE: Human-readable output rules are defined in `references/phases/decide.md`.
The brief must read like a strategy memo — no snake_case identifiers, no raw effect_ids.

---

```markdown
# Decision Brief: {decision_statement}

Generated: {date} | Iterations: {N} | Converged: {yes at iteration M / NOT REACHED / N/A (medium mode)} | Tilt: {tilt_name}

## Executive Summary

> **Decision:** {decision_statement — one line}
> **Recommendation:** {action — one line} | **Confidence:** {HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW} | Converged: {yes/no}
> **Hypotheses explored:** {N — one-line framing of what was in play}
> **Deepest disagreement:** {biggest council divergence and why it matters}
> **Dominant risk:** {top worst case or black swan — one line}
> **Load-bearing assumption:** {top HIGH-sensitivity assumption}

Readable in 30 seconds. Preview of the possibility map with the recommendation called out. No hedging. No "it depends." The full possibility map is in the body of the brief. The full recommendation — with action plan, dependencies, monitoring signals, pre-mortem, and review trigger — is in the Recommendation section at the end.

---

## Data Foundation

Assign every external fact a stable source tag that gets reused in every
downstream section of the brief. Three tag classes:

- **[G#]** — Ground data from Phase 1 WebSearch. Number sequentially: [G1], [G2], ...
  Each [G#] gets a URL in the Sources section.
- **[U#]** — User-provided fact from Phase 1.5 ELICIT. Numbered separately: [U1], [U2], ...
- **[C#:persona]** — Council estimate from Phase 3 personas (e.g., [C1:pessimist]).
  Used downstream when you cite a probability, range, or heuristic the council
  produced. Numbered separately.

Example:

> - [G1] Competitor X raised $200M Series C in Q3 2025 (source: Crunchbase).
> - [G2] SaaS price elasticity median -1.2 to -1.8 across B2B studies (source: ProfitWell 2024 report).
> - [U1] "Our current LTV:CAC is 3.1:1" (provided by user, Apr 2026).
> - [C1:pessimist] "Volume offset P=0.15 under base elasticity assumption."

Every specific number that appears later in the brief (dollar figures, percentages,
multipliers, elasticities) must cite one of these tags within 120 characters of the number.
If UNGROUNDED: "Warning: This analysis was not grounded in external data. Conclusions rely on LLM reasoning only."

## Effects Map

### High-Confidence Effects (council agreement >= 3)

These effects were independently generated by 3+ of 5 personas. Lead with these.

#### First-Order
| Effect | Probability | Range | Agreement | Timeframe | Key Assumptions |
|--------|------------|-------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| {description} | {P} | [{min}-{max}] | {N}/5 | {timeframe} | {assumptions} |

Validator reference IDs are collected in a **single trailing block** at the end of
the brief (`<!-- validator-refs: ... -->`), NOT inline on each row. Keeps the raw
markdown clean. See `references/phases/decide.md` Step 4c.

#### Second-Order
| Effect | Triggered By | Probability | Range | Agreement | Timeframe |
|--------|-------------|------------|-------|-----------|-----------|
| {description} | {parent} | {P} | [{min}-{max}] | {N}/5 | {timeframe} |

**Top-15 rule.** Sections 4's tables (High-Confidence + Specialist + Exploratory
combined) show the top 15 effects by `council_agreement × probability`. Every
other effect goes to **Appendix C: Complete Effects Map** at the end of the
brief. No effect is dropped — they're triaged. The possibility map is preserved;
the reader's scan is protected.

### Specialist Insights (council agreement 1, domain-expert match)

These effects were generated by a single persona whose domain expertise directly
matches the finding. They belong here, not in "exploratory," because the majority
SHOULD miss them — that's the whole point of having specialists.

The `[SPECIALIST: {tag}]` lead and the "Specialist" column both use the **short tag**
from `persona-council.md` "Canonical Persona Names" table (Optimist, Pessimist,
Competitor, Regulator, Customer). Never use the long name here.

| Effect | P | Specialist | Why Only They Saw It |
|--------|---|-----------|---------------------|
| [SPECIALIST: Regulator] {compliance finding} | {P} | Regulator | {domain match explanation} |
| [SPECIALIST: Competitor] {competitive dynamic} | {P} | Competitor | {domain match explanation} |

### Exploratory Effects (council agreement 1-2, no specialist match)

These effects were generated by 1-2 personas without a clear domain-expertise match.
They may be genuine blind spots OR noise. Treat with appropriate skepticism.

**Inline, in full.** Do NOT point to effects-chains.json — the JSON is for the
validator, not the reader. Every exploratory effect that didn't make the top 15
cutoff goes in the Appendix C table (position 15). Exploratory effects that DO
make the top 15 appear here in full.

| Effect | P | Agreement | Source Persona | Note |
|--------|---|-----------|---------------|------|
| {description} | {P} | {N}/5 | {persona} | {why it might matter — one specific sentence, not "may be relevant"} |

If exploratory effects in this table exceed 10 rows, the rest move to Appendix C.
Never summarize to "N additional exploratory effects" in the main body — that
erases the map. Appendix C is for volume; the main body is for the top.

## Council Dynamics

Who thought what, where the council diverged. This is the diversity signal — the reason to run a council rather than a single analyst. Do NOT compress this to one line.

**Legend (emit verbatim as first line of this section):**

> *Personas: Optimist (revenue/market share), Pessimist (capital preservation),
> Competitor (market response), Regulator (compliance/sustainability),
> Customer (user value/adoption). Tag format: [persona].*

This one line saves readers from having to remember what each label means. It
is MANDATORY — the validator checks for its presence.

- **Strongest analysis:** {persona} (per peer review) — {one line on why}
- **Weakest analysis:** {persona} — {one line on why: too generic, not grounded, etc.}
- **Key disagreement:** {what effect or hypothesis, and why personas split — include the probability range and which personas took which side}
- **Uncertainty hotspot:** {effect with widest probability range — show the range and which personas produced the min and max}
- **Consensus surprises:** {effects where unexpected personas agreed — e.g., Optimist and Pessimist aligned on X}
- **Blind spots caught:** {effects that only 1-2 personas saw but peer review elevated as credible}
- **Cross-order consensus (if any):** {effects where the cross-order dedup pass (simulate.md Step 3.5) found hidden agreement — e.g., "merchant churn" was 1st-order for Pessimist AND 2nd-order for Competitor, revealing 4/5 implicit agreement}

## Minority-View Winners

If the final recommendation was influenced or originated by a single persona's
creative alternative (an `alt_` effect or a hypothesis proposed by one persona),
list it here. This is the most compelling proof that the council added value.

Often, the winning recommendation comes from a single persona's non-obvious
alternative. These are the highest-leverage outputs of the system.

- **{winning idea}** — originated from {persona name}. Council agreement at origin: 1/5.
  Became the leading recommendation because: {why it won despite low initial agreement}.

If no minority-view idea influenced the final recommendation, omit this section.

## Adversarial Scenarios

Applied AFTER stable/fragile classification so the reader already knows what survived pressure before seeing what attacks it. Sourced from `adversary.json`. Every entry in the source JSON appears here — never drop a subsection that has source data.

**Reference IDs go in the single trailing `<!-- validator-refs: ... -->` block
at the end of the brief** — not inline per scenario. The validator in Phase 8.5
parses the trailing block and confirms scenario IDs are preserved.
See `references/phases/decide.md` Step 4c for the full rule.

### Worst Cases
{Top 2-3 worst case scenarios with probability and severity. Not just "bad outcomes" — specifically the scenarios where the primary recommendation is wrong AND costly. Every specific number in the scenario must carry a source tag.}

Example format:
```
- **Compound-negative 12-month death spiral.** Price cut fails (elasticity comes in
  at −0.9 not −1.4 [G2]), competitors match within 8 weeks [G1], enterprise churn
  accelerates, LTV:CAC lands at 2.0:1 [C3:pessimist]. P=0.15 [C3:pessimist],
  severity HIGH.
```

### Black Swans
{Low probability, high impact events. Unexpected disruptions (macro, security, regulatory, new entrant). 2-3 entries. Each as a paragraph with specific narrative — not a bullet.}

Example format:
```
**Hyperscaler acquisition.** A major tech platform acquires your strongest
competitor and bundles the product into their suite at zero marginal cost.
This changes competitive dynamics entirely and invalidates H1 and H3. P=0.10 [C4:competitor].
```

### Irrational Actors
{Scenarios where a competitor, regulator, or internal actor behaves in a way rational analysis would not predict. At least one. Rare but high-impact.}

Example format:
```
- **Competitor overreacts.** Rather than matching selectively on contested
  accounts, CEO treats the cut as existential threat and slashes 40% across
  the board. Triggers a margin-destroying price war. P(irrational)=0.20 [C4:competitor],
  impact HIGH.
```

## Recommendation

The synthesis of everything above. One path forward, based on expected value across the hypothesis space with worst cases and black swans factored in. This is a synthesis of the map, not the product of the analysis — the map above is the product.

- **Action:** {what to do}
- **Confidence:** {HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW}
- **Confidence reasoning:** {why this level — what the stable findings support and what the fragile findings caveat}
- **Depends on:** {top 1-2 load-bearing assumptions. **MUST mirror rows from the
  Key Assumptions table** (position 10). Every depends-on item must also appear
  in that table. If a depends-on item isn't in Key Assumptions, one of them is
  wrong — fix both. The validator enforces this.}
- **Monitor:** {specific signals that would change this recommendation — metrics, events, dates}
- **Pre-mortem:** {if this fails in 12 months, it will most likely be because... — 3-5 failure modes synthesized from Adversarial Scenarios + Fragile Insights}
- **Review trigger:** {when to re-run this analysis — a date, a metric threshold, or a named event}

## Appendix A: Decision Timeline

A month-by-month execution plan showing actions, dependencies, and kill criteria.

```
| When | Action | Depends On | Decision Point | Kill Criteria |
|------|--------|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| Week 1 | {first action} | — | — | — |
| Month 1 | {action} | {what must be done first} | — | — |
| Month 3 | {action} | {dependency} | {go/no-go decision} | {metric that kills this path} |
| Month 6 | {checkpoint} | {dependency} | {on-track if X} | {intervention if Y} |
| Month 12 | {review} | {dependency} | {renew/change} | {exit if Z} |
```

Rules:
- **Depends On** column shows what MUST complete before this action can start.
  Use specific prior actions, not vague prerequisites.
- **Kill Criteria** is the metric or event that stops this path. Be specific:
  "<20% WAU at month 3" not "low adoption."
- Include BOTH effects-driven events (competitor responds, churn materializes)
  AND execution actions (deploy pilot, ship connector, negotiate contract).
- Show causal arrows where a 2nd-order effect is triggered by a 1st-order
  effect at an earlier month.
```
