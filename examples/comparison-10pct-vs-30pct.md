## Key Tradeoffs

### Choosing 10% over 30%

**You gain:** Lower financial risk (3-7pt vs 10-20pt margin compression), no brand damage, easier reversibility, lower competitive alarm. The damage is contained.

**You lose:** Even the modest acquisition lift that 30% might generate in SMB. The 10% cut is firmly in the "worst of both worlds" — it costs real margin but doesn't move the needle enough to matter.

### Choosing 30% over 10%

**You gain:** Meaningful SMB acquisition lift (20-40% vs 5-15%), enough price delta to actually change enterprise procurement conversations, a decisive market signal.

**You lose:** Brand positioning (desperation signal), LTV (30%+ compounding destruction), pricing power for years (3-5x resistance to increases), fundraising narrative, NRR health. The downside is catastrophic and largely irreversible.

### Choosing neither (recommended by both analyses)

**You gain:** Preserved margins, brand, pricing power, and LTV. Freedom to pursue hybrid/usage-based restructuring that addresses the same growth goal with 38% revenue growth premium.

**You lose:** Short-term competitive price wins and the psychological satisfaction of "doing something." But the research is clear: the "something" destroys more value than it creates at either magnitude.

## Recommendation

**Neither cut is advisable, but if forced to choose, the 10% cut is significantly less damaging.**

The 10% cut is a mistake you can recover from (2-3x resistance to future increases, but margins survive). The 30% cut is a mistake that compounds (LTV destruction, permanent anchor reset, brand damage, NRR collapse). The 10% fails by being too small to matter; the 30% fails by being too large to survive.

**The actual recommendation from both analyses is the same:** restructure pricing (hybrid, usage-based, or tiered entry) rather than cutting. This captures price-sensitive demand without permanently reducing your price anchor, compressing margins, or signaling distress.

**If you want to test price sensitivity before committing:**
- Run a 90-day time-limited discount (10% for annual commitments) to measure elasticity empirically
- Create a new lower-cost entry tier targeting SMB without touching enterprise pricing
- Pilot a usage-based component on one product line
